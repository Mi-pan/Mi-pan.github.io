Wikipedia shows a perfect map. (On the map photo data on Wikipedia) As you can see, the expressway is similar to a spiderweb, which has a rule. All the expressways in this Kanto area (Tokyo and its surrounding 6 prefectures) consist of the routes that spreads from Tokyo toward suburbs, and the route that are circular and look like some concentric circles. Red roads are all Shutoko, and green roads are all different kind of expressways, which means transferring to those green roads while driving on Shutoko will cost you another highway toll besides the toll for Shutoko.

 

For the circular routes, from the center, inner 2 are C1(都心環状線) and C2(中央環状線) (both of these are Shutoko), and Gaikan(外環), Keno-do(圏央道) respectively (the last one is not on this map).

You should remember 3 words when driving on Japanese expressways (not only Shutoko but also for every expressway routes in Japan)

 

IC (Interchange = an entrance and an exit of an expressway route)

 

JCT (Junction = an intersection between expressways that you use to transfer to another expressway route)

 

PA (Parking Area = a facility with toilets and restaurants or souvenir shops besides the roads that you can use without getting off the expressway)

 

SA (Service Area = a large PA with even a gas stations sometimes)

 

Traffic rules of Shutoko

 

The speed limit on Shutoko is 50km/h on C1 (Inner Circular Route), 60km/h on most areas,  and 80km/h on B; Wangan-sen (Bayshore Route). However many cars tend to go over the speed limits. Nobody calls police for your being  over-speed,  so what you have to care about is Police car (black and white one, and unmarked one which looks like a normal sedan), Police motorcycle,  speed cameras, mobile speed cameras.

 

It is important for you to look at the back using the mirror of your car, as often as possible (don't be careless about the front of course! Keep your eyes on the front!) for your changing lanes and to detect the police car as early as possible. For your changing lanes, Shutoko's drivers are skillful, changing lanes with the distance between other cars so small and they tend to keep less distance when traveling compared to drivers in other rural areas in Japan, so you have better notice whether there are other cars around you, and its distance from you. It is said that speed cameras will work and be effective when you go over speed limit for 30km/h on normal public roads, and 40km/h on expressways, which means you won't be caught by a police even if you travel at 115km/h under the speed camera on Bayshore Route. However, an increasing number of mobile speed cameras have started to be used, and its location changes day to day. (This mobile speed camera is different from what we see outside the highways. On public roads in residential areas, this camera operates with a policemen next to this machine, so the proof of being over-speed is easy to be recorded and declared. It is said that this mobile speed camera on normal roads at a residential area will work and be effective when you go over a speed limit for 15 or 16km/h. Here, the police in charge of it will stop your car right after your breaking the speed limit, but on highways even police cannot stop your car because it's dangerous of course, so I guess the amount of over-speed that make you caught by police is different. A person must be over-speed by 30km or so if that person is not caught red-handed, caught in the act, I think(?) )

 

Speed camera (permanent one, not mobile one whose location moves day to day) on the side of the road can be searched on the internet. Look at this website: https://orbis-guide.com/ Speed cameras are called オービス; Orbis in Japanese. There are several types of it. Few decades ago, sometimes you were not caught by the police even if you pass the camera at 200km/h because of running out of a film inside the machine, but now every photos are taken digitally, and sent to police right away so you will be caught 100% (still there are some cases of being not caught because of the low quality, poor accuracy of the photo (to arrest someone, the driver's face must be clear on the photo) ). The websites and recently many Apps make it easy to detect speed cameras not only on Shutoko but everywhere in Japan. (On the roads, there is a machine called "N-system" that looks similar to the speed camera. N-system just aim to record our licence plate number, not the travelling speed so you don't have to care about it, but some people who don't know this slow down when passing through the machine...)

 

Police cars won't catch you for your being over-speed by 10km/h or so because most cars are over speed limits and that's the custom for us...

 

At every roads in Japan, if there are two or more lanes, we must not travel using right lanes if there are no cars to overtake or pass. However, in normal roads (roads that are everywhere and require no tolls), many cars that do not pass other cars or that do not even turn left, continue to drive at the left lane and this is one reason of the inefficiency of Japanese roads (many cars that want to drive fast will be stuck behind it).

 

However, Shutoko has it’s exits on both side and car merges from both side, so even if you keep staying  on the right lane, the police will almost not catch you for keep traveling on the wrong lane (you will be caught on any other expressways, so be careful) (but there is a case of a person being caught for this at Shutoko Bayshore route (Wangan sen) because there are exits on only left side just as a highway at a rural area, so try to stay at left side of the road if there’re no cars to pass or if there're no exits that you want to take at the right side)
